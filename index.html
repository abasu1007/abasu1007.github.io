Anirban Basu

Search form

Anirban got his Bachelor of Engineering (B.E.) from the Indian Institute of Engineering Science and Technology (IIEST) at Shibpur (formerly Bengal Engineering and Science University, Shibpur) in 2006. After graduation, he spent 2 years as a Software Engineer with an Indian IT consultancy firm. Here, he worked as a Datawarehousing and Data Mining consultant for Wachovia bank and developed tools for them to track money laundering and collateral as well as for migrating their data from a legacy system to a more robust server.

In 2008, Anirban left for the US to pursue advanced degrees in Electrical Engineering. He got his MS degree from University of Florida in 2010. At UF, Anirban worked with Dr Huikai Xie on fabrication of an ultra high density capacitor for low-power Systems-on-Chip. During the course of his MS, he also took an interest in MEMS, tiny micromechanical devices that are built using the manufacturing techniques from the Semiconductor industry.

Anirban moved to Northeastern University at Boston for his PhD on MEMS relays. Under Dr Nick McGruer and Dr George Adams, he worked on an AFM based test-bed for reliability assessment of metal contacts for MEMS relays. His thesis, titled "An experimental investigation of hot switching contact damage in RF MEMS switches", was published in 2014 and addresses unique reliability issues in MEMS that were not looked at before. Nanoscale quantum mechanisms that are usually not of importance in macro relays, were found to be extremely important in MEMS contacts.

In 2014, Anirban joined MIT as a postdoctoral associate to work on field electron emitter arrays. These are nanosharp MEMS-type devices which are capable of enhancing surface electric fields in order to enable electron emission. Anirban is currently working at Microsystems Technology Laboratories with Senior Research Scientist Luis Velásquez-García to build miniaturized vacuum systems that use low power electron emission to generate X-rays and to enable chip-scale atomic spectroscopy sensors - a collaborative work with Draper Laboratories, Cambridge.
