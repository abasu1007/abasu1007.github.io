Anirban Basu

Hi! Thank you for visiting my page!

My name is Anirban. I have been a researcher in the area of micro/nano electromechanical structures for last 8 years, and am a passionate engineer. Throughout my career, I have consciously attempted to gain expertise in a wide range of subjects. I have worked on projects that required hands-on experimental skills, I have built large scale systems, I have worked on projects that required strong analytical skills, worked on data analysis, system modeling, and characterization.

I pursued a Bachelor of Engineering (B.E.) degree at the Indian Institute of Engineering Science and Technology (IIEST) at Shibpur (earlier called Bengal Engineering and Science University). I majored in Electrical Engineering (with a final year project on power system stability) and after graduation started working as a Software Engineer with an Indian IT consultancy firm.

Over the next two years, I worked as a Datawarehousing and Data Mining consultant for Wachovia bank and developed tools for them to track money laundering and collateral as well as for migrating their data from a legacy system to a more robust server.

In 2008, I came to the US to pursue advanced degrees in Electrical Engineering. I got my MS degree from University of Florida in 2010. At UF, I worked on an array of projects ranging from signal processing, electromagnetic field simulations, micro-electromechanical structures (MEMS), and microelectronic fabrication. My Masters project with Dr Huikai Xie involved fabrication of an ultra high density capacitor for low-power Systems-on-Chip. Ultimately, for my PhD, I decided to work in MEMS since the interdisciplinary nature of this field intrigued me.

I moved to Northeastern University at Boston for my PhD on MEMS relays. Under Dr Nick McGruer and Dr George Adams, I worked on an atomic force microscope (AFM) based experimental setup for reliability assessment of metal contacts for MEMS relays. While working on my thesis, titled "An experimental investigation of hot switching contact damage in RF MEMS switches",I developed expertise in fields varying from circuit analysis, device modeling, data acquisition and statistical analysis, mechanical and thermal characterization of silicon and metal structures, as well as quantum mechanics in the context of microcontacts. My thesis was published in 2014 and addresses unique reliability issues in MEMS that were not looked at before. Nanoscale quantum mechanisms that are usually not of importance in macro relays, were found to be extremely important in MEMS contacts.

In 2014, I joined MIT as a postdoctoral associate to work on field electron emitter arrays. I simultaneously handled two projects on MEMS-type devices which are capable of enhancing surface electric fields that allow electron emission through quantum tunneling. I am currently working at Microsystems Technology Laboratories with Senior Research Scientist Luis Velásquez-García to build miniaturized vacuum systems that use low power electron emission to generate X-rays (a project where I collaborated with Mass General Hospital) and to enable chip-scale atomic spectroscopy sensors (a collaborative work with Draper Laboratories, Cambridge).
